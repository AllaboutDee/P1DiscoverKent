Discover the Rich History of Kent


Kent, often called the "Garden of England," is one of the most historically rich counties in the UK, with a history stretching back thousands of years. Its strategic location in southeastern England, close to continental Europe, has made it a significant region throughout British history, witnessing invasions, battles, and the growth of trade, agriculture, and industry.


Kent offers a blend of rich history and scenic landscapes. From medieval castles to charming towns, embark on a journey through time and explore Kent's unique attractions.Here’s an overview of Kent's history and some of its notable historical sites worth visiting:


Ancient Kent


Prehistoric Era: Kent has been inhabited since prehistoric times, as evidenced by archaeological finds like the Swanscombe Skull, a 400,000-year-old human fossil. Kent was rich in resources, attracting settlers who farmed the fertile land and hunted.


Iron Age and the Celts: By the Iron Age, Kent was inhabited by Celtic tribes. The tribe known as the Cantiaci (or Cantii) lived in the area, giving Kent its name.


Roman Invasion: In 43 AD, the Romans invaded Britain, landing on the Kent coast. They built roads and settlements, notably Durovernum Cantiacorum (modern-day Canterbury) and Dubris (Dover). Kent's location near the Continent made it an entry point for Roman forces.


Anglo-Saxon and Viking Periods


Anglo-Saxon Kingdom of Kent: After the Romans left Britain in the early 5th century, Kent became one of the earliest Anglo-Saxon kingdoms, established by the Jutes. It became a center for Christianity with the arrival of St. Augustine in 597 AD, who converted King Æthelberht of Kent and established a church in Canterbury, marking the beginning of England's Christian heritage.

Viking Raids: From the 8th century, Kent was frequently raided by Vikings. The area around Thanet and the Thames estuary suffered from Norse invasions, leading to a period of instability.


Medieval Kent


Norman Conquest: After the Norman invasion of 1066, Kent continued to play an important role as the "gateway to England." William the Conqueror built fortifications to secure the region, including Rochester Castle and Dover Castle, which became essential to his rule.


Canterbury Pilgrimage: In the medieval period, Canterbury became a significant pilgrimage destination after the murder of Thomas Becket in Canterbury Cathedral in 1170. Geoffrey Chaucer’s The Canterbury Tales, written in the 14th century, describes pilgrims traveling from London to Canterbury to visit Becket's shrine.


Hundred Years' War: Kent was frequently on high alert during the Hundred Years' War due to its proximity to France. Dover Castle was fortified further as protection against French invasion attempts.


Tudor and Stuart Kent


Tudor Era: During Henry VIII’s reign, Kent saw further development. Henry built several coastal forts in the region as part of his defensive strategy, including Deal, Sandown, and Walmer Castles. He also dissolved many of the monasteries in the county, including the great St. Augustine's Abbey in Canterbury.


Civil War: Kent was an important region during the English Civil War in the 17th century. While much of the county initially supported Parliament, a significant Royalist uprising occurred in 1648, which ended with the Battle of Maidstone.


Georgian and Victorian Kent


Agricultural Growth: Kent's fertile soil contributed to its nickname, the "Garden of England," as it became famous for growing hops, apples, and cherries, essential for the production of cider and beer. The county’s agriculture flourished, supplying London and other urban centers.


Industrial Era: In the 19th century, the development of the railways connected Kent with London, leading to the growth of towns like Maidstone, Ashford, and Chatham. Kent also became a center for shipbuilding, especially around the Chatham Dockyard.


20th Century and World War II


World War I: Due to its location, Kent was often the first place affected by German air raids and bombings.


World War II: Kent played a critical role in WWII. The Battle of Britain was fought in the skies over Kent, and the area around Dover became known as "Hellfire Corner" due to the heavy shelling and bombing it endured. Many defensive structures, including pillboxes and bunkers, can still be seen around Kent.


Post-War Development: Following the war, Kent grew as both an agricultural and industrial region. It also became increasingly popular for its scenic countryside and historic sites, leading to growth in tourism.
