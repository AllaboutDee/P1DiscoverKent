Explore Kent's Historical Sites by Town

Kent is home to many historical sites, each with unique stories and cultural significance. Browse by town below to discover heritage sites, museums, and landmarks.

Discover Kent’s Rich Historical Tapestry

Kent, known as the "Garden of England," is a region steeped in centuries of history, from Roman fortresses and medieval castles to the landmarks of English literary and religious heritage. Each town tells its own story, preserving a unique piece of England’s past. Whether you're following in the footsteps of ancient pilgrims, exploring battlements that once protected the coast, or marveling at architectural treasures, Kent offers countless historical sites for exploration. Browse the towns below to uncover the stories behind Kent’s most cherished sites and plan your journey through time.

Maidstone

Maidstone, the county town of Kent, was a major medieval market center for agriculture and the cloth trade. During the English Civil War, it saw a notable battle between Royalist forces and Parliamentary troops.

Places to Visit:

  • Leeds Castle: Often called the "loveliest castle in the world," Leeds Castle has been a royal residence since the 13th century.
    • Learn More
  • Kent Museum of Rural Life: This museum showcases the rural lifestyle and agricultural practices that defined Kent for centuries. These buildings include traditional farmhouses, oast houses, and a village hall, all meticulously restored and furnished to reflect their respective eras and these are situated within Kent Life Farm.
    • Learn More

Canterbury

Canterbury is renowned for its religious history and the Canterbury Cathedral, a UNESCO World Heritage site. After the murder of Archbishop Thomas Becket in 1170, it became a major pilgrimage site.

Places to Visit:

  • Canterbury Cathedral: Headquarters of the Church of England, known for its stained glass and Becket’s Shrine.
  • St Augustine's Abbey: Founded in 598, this abbey marks the spread of Christianity in England.
  • Westgate Towers: A medieval gatehouse with views over the historic town.
Learn More

Ashford

A Roman settlement that grew into a significant market town, Ashford became an industrial center in the 19th century thanks to the railway.

Places to Visit:

  • Godinton House and Gardens: A 14th-century house surrounded by beautiful gardens, showcasing historic English country life.
  • Ashford Museum: Displays artifacts tracing Ashford's transformation from Roman times to the railway era.
Learn More

Dartford

Dartford served as a crossing point on the River Thames since ancient times. It’s known for its connections to medieval pilgrimages.

Places to Visit:

  • Dartford Borough Museum: This museum showcases the town's history from the Stone Age through to its industrial heritage.
  • St. Edmund’s Pleasance: Once home to a leper hospital, the site offers a quiet place of reflection.
Learn More

Dover

Dover, famous for its white cliffs, has been a strategic port and defense site for over 2,000 years.

Places to Visit:

  • Dover Castle: Known as the "Key to England," it has medieval tunnels, a Roman lighthouse, and WWII bunkers.
  • Roman Painted House: The best-preserved Roman house in England, with original wall paintings.
  • Dover Museum: Known for its Bronze Age Boat, one of the oldest known seagoing boats.
Learn More

Folkestone

Folkestone transitioned from a small fishing village to a popular Victorian seaside resort and an embarkation point during both World Wars.

Places to Visit:

  • Leas Lift: A historic funicular railway that offers scenic views of the coast.
  • Folkestone Harbour Arm: Once a busy ferry terminal, now a vibrant cultural space with cafes, art installations, and music.
Learn More

Gravesend

Known for the burial of Pocahontas, who died here in 1617, Gravesend has a history in maritime trade.

Places to Visit:

  • St. George's Church: The resting place of Pocahontas, featuring a statue in her honor.
  • New Tavern Fort: An 18th-century defense against potential French invasions, with bunkers and tunnels.
Learn More

Gillingham

Gillingham's Royal Dockyard supported the Royal Navy for centuries, contributing to its growth during the 17th and 18th centuries.

Places to Visit:

  • Royal Engineers Museum: Showcases the engineering feats and innovations of the British Army's Royal Engineers.
  • Gillingham Pier: Offers views over the River Medway and historical context of the local shipbuilding industry.
Learn More

Margate

Margate became one of Britain’s earliest seaside resorts, attracting artists like J.M.W. Turner.

Places to Visit:

  • Turner Contemporary: A contemporary art gallery celebrating Turner’s legacy in the town.
  • Shell Grotto: An underground maze adorned with over 4.6 million shells of unknown origins.
  • Dreamland: A vintage amusement park with a retro vibe, capturing the spirit of early British seaside holidays.
Learn More

Ramsgate

Ramsgate's harbor was granted royal status by King George IV, marking its importance in trade and naval history.

Places to Visit:

  • Ramsgate Maritime Museum: Chronicles the maritime history of the town and its role in the Napoleonic Wars.
  • St. Augustine’s Church: Designed by Augustus Pugin, this neo-Gothic gem is a pilgrimage site and architecturally significant.
Learn More

Sevenoaks

A prosperous market town, Sevenoaks became a retreat for wealthy Londoners who built grand estates here.

Places to Visit:

  • Knole House and Park: A grand Tudor mansion with 1,000 acres of parkland, once home to Henry VIII.
  • Riverhill Himalayan Gardens: Historic gardens with an international plant collection and views over the Kent Downs.
Learn More

Sittingbourne

Once an important industrial hub, Sittingbourne thrived with the paper and brick industries.

Places to Visit:

  • Sittingbourne and Kemsley Light Railway: A preserved railway originally used for paper production.
  • Bredgar & Wormshill Light Railway: A private steam railway offering scenic rides through the Kent countryside.
Learn More

Tonbridge

Known for its castle, Tonbridge was a Norman stronghold during the 13th-century baronial conflicts.

Places to Visit:

  • Tonbridge Castle: Offers a Norman castle keep with medieval exhibitions.
  • Haysden Country Park: A riverside park with trails, lakes, and connections to the historic Medway River.
Learn More

Tunbridge Wells

Founded as a spa town when Chalybeate Springs were discovered in the 17th century.

Places to Visit:

  • The Pantiles: A historic colonnade of shops and cafes, named after its distinctive tiled paving.
  • Tunbridge Wells Museum & Art Gallery: Displays collections on the town's history and its fashionable rise as a spa destination.
Learn More

Chatham

The Royal Dockyard here was a significant center for shipbuilding and naval defense.

Places to Visit:

  • Historic Dockyard Chatham: Includes warships, historic buildings, and museums.
  • Fort Amherst: Built to protect the dockyard, this is the largest Napoleonic fortress in Britain.
Learn More

Aylesford

Aylesford is known for its 13th-century Carmelite Priory, established in 1242. The town also has connections to the Battle of Aylesford in 455 AD, which was part of the Anglo-Saxon invasion.

Places to Visit:

  • Aylesford Priory: One of the oldest Carmelite establishments in the country, this peaceful location offers beautiful grounds, a medieval chapel, and an extensive collection of religious artifacts.
  • Teapot Island: An eccentric museum on the River Medway featuring over 8,000 teapots in various designs, adding charm and humor to this historic village.
Learn More

Broadstairs

Known for its Victorian seaside appeal, Broadstairs was beloved by Charles Dickens, who frequented the town in the 19th century.

Places to Visit:

  • Dickens House Museum: Once the home of a friend of Charles Dickens, the museum offers a look into Dickensian life and inspiration for his novels.
  • Bleak House: Overlooking Viking Bay, this was where Dickens wrote *David Copperfield*. The house is a charming spot with incredible views and historical exhibits.
Learn More

Cranbrook

Once called the "Capital of the Weald," Cranbrook became a center of the wool and cloth trade in the medieval period.

Places to Visit:

  • Union Mill: A large and well-preserved windmill from 1814, still working and open to visitors.
  • Cranbrook Museum: A quaint museum that delves into the local history, especially the town’s role in the wool trade.
Learn More

Deal

Known as a smuggling hotspot in the 18th century, Deal also served as an essential naval base.

Places to Visit:

  • Deal Castle: Built by Henry VIII as a coastal defense, this well-preserved castle has intricate passageways and cannon rooms.
  • Timeball Tower: Used in the 19th century to signal the correct time to passing ships; it’s an unusual structure with maritime heritage exhibits.
Learn More

Edenbridge

A medieval market town known for its rural agriculture and fairs, Edenbridge still retains much of its historic charm.

Places to Visit:

  • Hever Castle: The childhood home of Anne Boleyn, Henry VIII’s second wife, filled with Tudor history, beautiful gardens, and a moat.
  • Eden Valley Museum: Located in a 14th-century building, offering artifacts from local history, including Roman finds and WWI memorabilia.
Learn More

Faversham

Known for its breweries and the gunpowder industry, Faversham has long been a market town with a history that goes back to Roman times.

Places to Visit:

  • Shepherd Neame Brewery: Britain’s oldest brewery, with tours offering a look into traditional brewing techniques.
  • Chart Gunpowder Mills: Once part of a major industry, the mills now provide a glimpse into the manufacturing of gunpowder in the 17th century.
Learn More

Herne Bay

Herne Bay developed as a seaside town in the early 19th century, attracting holidaymakers with its pier and promenade.

Places to Visit:

  • Herne Bay Pier: The historic pier offers a funfair, seaside views, and small artisan shops.
  • The Seaside Museum: Provides insight into the maritime history of Herne Bay, featuring exhibits from local fossils to Victorian beach life.
Learn More

Hythe

One of the original Cinque Ports, Hythe has a history of maritime trade, piracy, and fishing.

Places to Visit:

  • St. Leonard’s Church: Famous for its ossuary, one of only two in England, containing the bones of thousands.
  • Royal Military Canal: Built as a defense during the Napoleonic Wars, the canal now provides a peaceful walking path.
Learn More

Lydd

This ancient town flourished in medieval times due to its wool trade. Known as one of the oldest towns in Kent, Lydd has historic churches and marshland landscapes.

Places to Visit:

  • All Saints Church: Known as the "Cathedral of the Marsh," this grand 15th-century church has distinctive architecture and medieval brasses.
Learn More

Minster-in-Thanet

Minster was an early religious center founded by St. Mildred and became influential in Anglo-Saxon Christianity.

Places to Visit:

  • Minster Abbey: Dating back to 670 AD, Minster Abbey is one of England’s oldest religious sites and a serene spot for contemplation.
  • St. Mary's Church: Another historic site in the village, known for its striking architecture and connection to the abbey.
Learn More

New Romney

Another of the Cinque Ports, New Romney has seen everything from Roman settlers to devastating storms that altered the coastline.

Places to Visit:

  • St. Nicholas Church: A Norman church once overlooking the harbor, now several miles inland due to historic flooding.
  • Romney, Hythe and Dymchurch Railway: Known as the world’s smallest public railway, it offers scenic rides through the Romney Marsh.
Learn More

Paddock Wood

Developed in the 19th century as a railway town, particularly famous for hop and fruit farming.

Places to Visit:

  • Hop Farm Country Park: Formerly the largest hop farm in the world, it now offers family activities and history of Kent’s brewing and hop-picking traditions.
  • Mascalls Gallery: A small gallery that hosts a range of contemporary and historic art exhibitions.
Learn More

Queenborough

Originally established as a fortification by Edward III, Queenborough served a defensive role on the Isle of Sheppey.

Places to Visit:

  • Queenborough Castle: Although the castle is mostly ruins, it holds historical importance as a royal fortress.
  • Queenborough Harbour: A peaceful harbor with scenic boat rides and connections to maritime trade.
Learn More

Rochester

An ancient city with Norman roots, Rochester was a cultural center that inspired Charles Dickens.

Places to Visit:

  • Rochester Castle: One of the best-preserved Norman castles in England, known for its tall keep and views over the Medway.
  • Rochester Cathedral: Founded in 604 AD, it’s England’s second oldest cathedral, with beautiful architecture and stained glass.
  • Guildhall Museum: Offers exhibits on the city’s history, from Roman artifacts to Victorian times.
Learn More

Sandwich

As one of the Cinque Ports, Sandwich was a medieval hub for trade, with well-preserved streets and buildings.

Places to Visit:

  • Sandwich Guildhall Museum: The town hall has fascinating displays on Sandwich’s rich history and the guildhall itself is of historical interest.
  • St. Peter’s Church: A blend of Norman and Tudor architecture with a peaceful garden and a bell tower visitors can climb.
Learn More

Sheerness

Sheerness developed around its naval dockyard, established in the 17th century and vital for the Royal Navy.

Places to Visit:

  • Blue Town Heritage Centre: Offers a history of Sheerness, with exhibitions on the dockyard and local heritage.
  • Sheerness Dockyard Church: Though partly in ruins, the church is historically significant as part of the dockyard community.
Learn More

Snodland

Known for its medieval roots, Snodland grew during the industrial revolution due to its thriving paper production.

Places to Visit:

  • Holborough Marshes: A nature reserve with trails that highlight the biodiversity of the Medway Valley.
  • All Saints Church: This historic church dates back to the Saxon era and contains beautiful Norman carvings.
Learn More

Staplehurst

A market village connected to the cloth industry, with a rural, picturesque setting.

Places to Visit:

  • Staplehurst Church: A historic building with connections to the medieval cloth trade.
  • Sissinghurst Castle Gardens: Famous for its beautiful gardens created by writer Vita Sackville-West, a short drive away.
Learn More

Swanley

Once a small agricultural village, Swanley expanded rapidly with the railway.

Places to Visit:

  • Swanley Park: Offers boating lakes, scenic trails, and a miniature railway.
  • Lullingstone Castle: One of England’s oldest family estates with the World Garden, featuring plants from around the globe.
Learn More

Tenterden

Known for its wool trade, Tenterden was a thriving market town in the medieval period.

Places to Visit:

  • Kent & East Sussex Railway: A heritage railway running through the scenic countryside.
  • Chapel Down Winery: A popular vineyard that offers tours and tastings of locally produced wines.
Learn More

West Malling

Known for its abbey founded in the 11th century, West Malling grew around its religious center.

Places to Visit:

  • St. Leonard’s Tower: An 11th-century Norman keep that offers a glimpse into medieval defense structures.
  • West Malling Abbey: Still an active nunnery with beautiful architecture and gardens.
Learn More

Westerham

Westerham was historically an agricultural town that became significant due to its connections to General James Wolfe and Sir Winston Churchill.

Places to Visit:

  • Chartwell House: Former home of Winston Churchill, with a rich history and his preserved study.
  • Quebec House: Birthplace of General Wolfe, hero of the Battle of Quebec.
Learn More

Whitstable

Known for its historic oyster trade, Whitstable is a fishing village with a lively maritime culture.

Places to Visit:

  • Whitstable Harbour: A vibrant spot where visitors can experience the traditional fishing culture.
  • Whitstable Castle: Known for its gardens and tea rooms, ideal for a relaxing afternoon.
  • Oyster Yards: The birthplace of Whitstable’s famous oysters, with opportunities to sample them locally.
